STRETCHWORK:
MANAGING THE CAREER PROGRESSION PARADOX IN
EXTERNAL LABOR MARKETS

SIOBHAN O’MAHONY
Harvard University

BETH A. BECHKY
University of California, Davis

Changes in employment relationships have diminished the degree to which internal
labor markets shape careers. Using comparative field studies, we examine how con-
tract workers try to achieve career progression without the benefit of organizational
guidance. Specifically, we examine how contract workers manage the career progres-
sion paradox: the problem of finding a job without prior experience. “Stretchwork”
bridging from proven competencies to new ones helps reconcile this paradox. We
identify four tactics used to acquire stretchwork, explore the conditions affecting the
success of those tactics, and offer theoretical implications for career progression in
external labor markets.

Studies of careers are essential to informing the
relationship between organizational structure and
individual action (Hughes, 1958/1981), but the
concept of career has usually been studied against
the backdrop of an organization structured by in-
ternal labor markets. Traditionally, a career was
associated with long-term employment with a sin-
gle employer and movement through a sequence of
increasingly challenging jobs within a hierarchy
(Tolbert, 1996; Wilensky, 1961). This concept has
dominated the notion of employment largely be-
cause most organizational structures have sup-
ported it (Sullivan, 1999). However, changes in the
postindustrial workplace suggest that both the
number of firms supporting traditional careers and
the number of people seeking such careers is grow-
ing smaller (Pfeffer & Baron, 1988).
The growth of outsourcing and contingent work
(Belous, 1989; Cohany, 1996), continuous growth
in self-employment (Steinmetz & Wright, 1989),

and a widespread shift to project work (e.g., Bielby
& Bielby, 1999; Hinds & Bailey, 2003) threaten the
relevance of the traditional career model. Tradi-
tional career theories contain four assumptions that
are not reflected in the environment of the last two
decades: stable organizations in stable environ-
ments, an intraorganizational focus, career mobility
from the standpoint of hierarchical positions, and
mobility within large organizations (Arthur, 1994).
Given the prevalence of interorganizational mobil-
ity, shorter organizational tenures, and horizontal
and lateral career moves (Nicholson & West, 1988;
Podolny & Baron, 1997; Stroh & Reilly, 1997), ex-
ternal labor markets are increasingly important
(Cappelli, 1999). However, career theory has not
kept pace with these structural changes in the
workplace.
In recognition of the changing nature of postin-
dustrial careers, a literature on boundaryless ca-
reers has emerged (e.g., Arthur & Rousseau, 1996).
Boundaryless careers do not unfold in a single or-
ganization but comprise less structured sequences
of jobs that may cross occupational, organizational,
and geographical boundaries (Arthur & Rousseau,
1996). Although this research has done much to
call attention to changes in the nature of careers,
the boundaryless metaphor may not be helpful in
examining the biggest challenge for people manag-
ing postindustrial careers: how to navigate a pro-
gressive series of jobs in a complex and volatile
external labor market. Faced with numerous career
choices and lacking an organizational framework
that helps to link those choices in a progressive

To further a grounded theory of careers in exter-
nal labor markets, we invoke a dilemma: the career
progression paradox. Workers encounter the career
progression paradox when they try to acquire work
to develop their skills in new areas but find that
employers prefer those with continuous prior ex-
perience. How can individuals acquire new expe-
riences without prior exposure to a job? This para-
dox is relevant for all workers making career
transitions, but it is exacerbated for contract work-
ers who are repeatedly hired for a specific duration
or purpose and thus face this challenge in external
labor markets more frequently. Although employ-
ers have few incentives to offer contract workers
jobs that might develop their skills, contract work-
ers have every incentive to expand their repertoires
of marketable skills.
Thus, we ask: How do contract workers achieve
progression in external labor markets? By compar-
ing the findings of field studies in two industries,
we identify the concept of stretchwork, which is
work that largely fits with an individual’s previous
work experience but introduces a small novel ele-
ment that extends his or her skills in a new direc-
tion. Our field data show that contract workers use
stretchwork assignments to reconcile the career
progression paradox. We identify four tactics used
to acquire stretchwork, explore the conditions af-
fecting those tactics, and offer implications to fur-
ther understanding of career progression in exter-
nal labor markets.

RELEVANT PERSPECTIVES ON CAREERS

Career theory is really not a unified theory but an
amalgamation of perspectives drawing on psychol-
ogy,
sociology,
anthropology,
and
economics
(Arthur, Hall, & Lawrence, 1989; Hall, 1976; Van
Maanen, 1977). All attempts to explain the unfold-
ing of human work experience over time can be
considered career theories (Arthur et al., 1989).
These perspectives share a focus on the evolving,
intertwined relationship between individual work
experience and its institutional context. Thus, one
way to understand career progression is to examine
how careers are organized with respect to the struc-
tures within which they develop.

Career Progression within Organizations

Within organizations, internal labor markets play
a dominant role in patterning individual careers
and operate under tacit assumptions of exchange
that attach the aspirations of workers to firms

(Arthur, 1994). Internal labor markets allocate la-
bor, determine wages, and define the scope of spe-
cific jobs according to sets of rules and procedures
(Doeringer & Piore, 1971). Employees are rewarded
with salary increases and promotions along inter-
nal job ladders in return for their loyalty and ex-
tended employment (Baker, Gibbs, & Holmstrom,
1994; Grandjean, 1981; Osterman, 1984). These
structures help foster a shared understanding of
how one job follows the next and the associated
skill requirements (Lawrence, 1990).
This arrangement has advantages for firms and
employees. Firms gain from reducing labor market
uncertainties by ensuring a readily available labor
supply (Osterman, 1984; Stark, 1986) and from al-
lowing themselves the time to earn a return on their
investment in developing their employees. Em-
ployees gain some protection from market risk be-
cause internal labor markets mediate wages, hiring,
and career development (Althauser, 1989; Baker &
Holmstrom, 1995). However, career patterns in or-
ganizations develop not just from the objective
structures of internal labor markets, but also from
the subjective inferences individuals make as firms
renew their management structures (Gunz, 1989:
42). As individuals develop their career paths, they
interpret signals sent by an organization’s prior de-
cisions, and these interpretations subsequently in-
fluence perceptions of what forms of action are
appropriate
for
their
progress
(e.g.,
Martin
&
Strauss, 1956). For instance, in the utility studied
by Lawrence (1984, 1990), managers developed
shared perceptions of their firm’s career timetable;
these perceptions influenced satisfaction, motiva-
tion, and promotion preferences.
Internal labor markets thus send powerful cues
about expected behaviors that can affect individual
preferences and courses of action (March, 1994). A
firm’s needs, encoded in its internal labor market,
structure a sequenced set of developmental oppor-
tunities for individuals (Gunz, 1989) and provide
incentives for both individuals and the firm to
work toward that progression. Internal labor mar-
kets provide workers with resources for action;
they also influence individual aspirations by pro-
jecting desirable ends, identifying a means to reach
those ends, and providing feedback on goal attain-
ment. Such resources for guiding career develop-
ment are less available in external labor markets,
where individuals may need to take more respon-
sibility to achieve progression. In the absence of
career ladders to sequence jobs, individuals must
develop strategies to craft their own careers. As a
result, career progression in external labor markets
may be less predictable.

Career Progression within External Labor
Markets

How careers are structured in external labor mar-
kets is less well understood than career progression
in organizations. Research has shown that, for in-
stance, some individuals are more locally oriented
toward the organizations in which they work,
while others are oriented toward a more expansive
social milieu or reference group (Gouldner, 1968;
Hughes, 1958/1981). However, as Zuckerman and
colleagues pointed out, “There exist no general
models for the sequences of shifts among jobs or
sectors that govern experience in any external labor
market” (Zuckerman, Kim, Ukanwa, & von Ritt-
mann, 2003: 1022). Although organizational ca-
reers have not disappeared (Jacoby, 1999), given
the rising importance of external labor markets
(Cappelli, 1999), an increasing number of theorists
have questioned the relevance of a consistent or-
ganizational framework as a backdrop to career
progression.
Scholars who have explored the notion of a
boundaryless career jettison key assumptions made
by traditional career theorists to reconcile theory
with postindustrial realities (Arthur, 1994; Arthur
& Rousseau, 1996). However, Gunz, Evans, and Jal-
land (2000) concluded that this metaphor over-
states the degree to which external labor markets
are unstructured. For instance, several empirical
studies of market-based careers have proposed that
networks structure external labor markets within
certain industries (Faulkner & Anderson, 1987;
Jones, 1996; Jones & Walsh, 1997). In their research
in the film industry, Faulkner and Anderson
showed how career opportunities differed for those
in the core versus the periphery of the industry’s
external labor market network (1987); Jones (1996)
further described the process by which the film
industry network constrained and enabled career
opportunities. Nevertheless, no one disputes that
careers in external labor markets are more unpre-
dictably structured than careers in internal labor
markets and that they therefore require people to
exert more control over their careers.
Scholars suggest two considerations that are par-
ticularly important for people who repeatedly seek
their futures in an external market: employability
and coherence. Moving from job to job and firm to
firm requires workers to concern themselves with
“employability”: the ability to market oneself in
external labor markets (Arthur & Rousseau, 1996;
Kanter, 1989). In external labor markets, employers
do not reward loyalty with promotion, but instead
provide opportunities to individuals with experi-
ences in diverse work settings (Kanter, 1989). Indi-

viduals construct their employability by develop-
ing
multiple
competencies
through
on-the-job
learning and by connecting to professional and so-
cial networks (Arthur & Rousseau, 1996; Higgins,
2001; Sullivan, 1999). Because workers navigating
external markets have no job ladders to help chart
their courses, they presumably hedge their bets by
seeking opportunities to learn skills that transfer to
other settings or that expose them to diverse social
networks. Researchers have argued that a greater
diversity of work settings enhances a person’s abil-
ity to acquire work from a greater number of em-
ployers. Although this literature advocates mobil-
ity across work settings, how workers achieve such
mobility remains largely unexamined.
Researchers have also argued that employers pre-
fer workers who demonstrate coherent sets of ex-
periences congruent with preestablished market
categories. Labor market classification schemes are
culturally and socially constituted and segment the
market in meaningful ways (White, 1981). The
more that people perceive the same market catego-
ries, the more relevant these categories will be, and
the more they will create career advantages for
individuals who fit them. For instance, Zuckerman
and colleagues (2003) found that Hollywood film
actors with coherent identities were more success-
ful at repeatedly obtaining work than those with
more fractured identities.
Individuals may foster coherence by drawing on
selected experiences to enhance the appearance of
continuity and ameliorate perceptions of disconti-
nuity with market categories (Ibarra, 2004; Ibarra &
Lineback, 2005). Those who defy prevailing cate-
gories may incur penalties, experiencing fewer op-
portunities and reduced earnings (Zuckerman,
1999; Zuckerman et al., 2003). In other words, the
appearance of coherence and conformity to market
categories pays. However, it is not clear what ac-
tions individuals should take to achieve or sustain
coherence when developing the skills necessary for
progressing to a new job or line of work.

The Career Progression Paradox

Indeed, to the extent that skills are acquired
through practice and experience, contract workers
face a catch-22: they need to have skills to land
jobs, but to learn the skills they have to first have
the jobs. We call this well-known dilemma the
career progression paradox. When employers have
no knowledge of a person’s capabilities or poten-
tial, they use his or her prior work experience as a
crude screening mechanism (Faulkner, 1983; Gunz
et al., 2000; Zuckerman et al., 2003). All workers
confront this paradox to some degree, but internal

labor markets ameliorate the problem (e.g., Becker,
1992). Because employers have opportunities to
observe employees’ personal characteristics and
learning habits (Pfeffer & Baron, 1988), in general,
offering a new work opportunity to an employee
should seem less risky than hiring an unknown.
Furthermore, if an employee has valuable firm-
specific knowledge, managers may be even more
willing to tolerate loss from on-the-job training.
Well-defined job ladders, investments in training,
firm-specific knowledge, trust, and a longer-term
investment horizon are all reasons for employers to
let employees perform work for which they lack
prior experience.
Contract workers face an entirely different situa-
tion. Most contract workers are strangers to the firm
at which they are working, have no firm-specific
knowledge, and have had no opportunity to earn
trust. Because the employment relationship is, by
definition, contingent on workers’ fit with an as-
signment and expected to be short term, employers
have no incentive to develop a temporary worker’s
potential or to hire someone who lacks necessary
skills and experience (Kunda, Barley, & Evans,
2002; Lepak & Snell, 1999; Marler, Barringer, &
Milkovich, 2002;). Furthermore, because contract
workers change jobs frequently, they are more
likely to encounter situations in which they try to
acquire jobs for which they lack prior experience.
The career progression paradox is thus more salient
for contract workers, who must draw on different
resources than are available to more permanent
workers to resolve this paradox and move forward
in their careers.
In the absence of organizational guidance, what
do contract workers do to progress in external labor
markets? Although employability and coherence
may well be goals that contract workers hope to
achieve by the moves they make, precisely how
workers achieve or project either remains an open
question. To move in the direction of a theory that
explains the structuring of market-based careers
requires analyzing and examining what indepen-
dent workers do to obtain positions that facilitate
progression. Thus, we investigated how contract
workers try to acquire not just another work assign-
ment, but one that diverges from their prior work
experience.

RESEARCH METHODS

Weick (1974) advocated that organizational re-
searchers attend not only to what they theorize
about, but also to where they do it. He encouraged
researchers to focus on places below the radar of
the sponsors of traditional organizational research,

places he termed “elsewheres” (Weick, 1974). In
such settings, some phenomena may be more visi-
ble and available for hypothesis generation than
they would be in settings with deeply entrenched
routines. To initiate a grounded theoretical concep-
tion of how workers in external markets resolve the
career progression paradox, we selected two con-
tract work settings that met this criterion. Neither
setting offered formal career development services
or training and, in both, contract workers sought to
expand their experience bases to new areas, know-
ing that their ability to acquire future work would
depend more on their past experiences than on
their capabilities or desires.
Following a strategy of theoretical sampling
(Strauss & Corbin, 1990), we compared contract
workers in the film industry, a field characterized
by stable roles, with contract workers in the high-
technology industry, which featured less stability
in roles and tasks. We sampled on this dimension
to develop theory across two external labor market
settings that differed in the degree to which they
resembled internal labor markets. Film production
takes place in single-project organizations marked
by short-term contractual or subcontractual rela-
tions (Faulkner & Anderson, 1987; Menger, 1999;
Mirvis & Hall, 1994). Despite the fact that film
projects differ from set to set, the content and or-
dering of roles are well established (Jones, 1996). A
grip who does mechanical work on a blockbuster
film will perform similar work on an independent
drama. Thus, film production crew work in an ex-
ternal labor market, but it is one guided by strong
roles and norms that in some respects resembles an
internal labor market.
In contrast, jobs in high-technology contracting
vary considerably from setting to setting, even
though they may share the same title. For example,
an application developer for a bank might engage in
very different work from an application developer
for an online retailer. This variance in jobs and
technology means that the careers of high-technol-
ogy contractors resemble organizational careers
even less than do the careers of film crew members.
Any theory generated by studying work in diver-
gent settings is more likely to be robust.

Differences. Although these two settings shared

Analytic Approach

Since little is known about the tactics that con-
tract workers use to craft careers in external labor
markets, a qualitative, inductive approach was well
suited for studying such tactics (Edmondson & Mc-
Manus, in press; Lee, 1999). We used grounded
analytic methods to analyze the data. The tech-
nique involved comparing and contrasting specific
instances in our field notes or interviews to build
theoretical categories that served as the basis for the
analysis (Glaser & Strauss, 1967; Strauss & Corbin,
1990). Few ethnographers “have attempted to col-
laborate across settings” and therefore “there are no
established
guidelines
for
how
ethnographers
should pool data” (Barley, 1996: 12). However, in a
study of technicians’ work, Barley (1996) provided
a detailed model of comparative ethnographic anal-
ysis that we followed in this study.
This process entailed individually conducting
emic analyses of our field data to allow informants’
understandings of their situations to emerge. We
subsequently compared the emic ethnographies
from each setting to create etic categories that re-
lated to more generalizable social science concepts.
Appendix B details this two-stage analysis. This
process enabled us to develop the theory below,
which is both grounded in data and relevant to the
literature on labor markets and careers.

TACTICS FOR RECONCILING THE CAREER
PROGRESSION PARADOX IN EXTERNAL
LABOR MARKETS

In both of our settings, we found that acquiring
stretchwork was the most common approach for
reconciling the career progression paradox. After
further defining stretchwork and showing how it
helped contract workers resolve the career progres-
sion paradox, we identify four tactics our infor-
mants used to acquire stretchwork that were robust
to our settings. We then specify boundary condi-
tions and link them with the tactics in a model that

shows how contract workers reconcile the career
progression paradox.

Thus, success in the quest to acquire stretchwork

There is a large literature examining the impor-
tance of referrals and social networks for obtain-
ing employment opportunities (e.g., Fernandez,
Castilla, & Moore, 2000; Granovetter, 1973, 1974).
Our findings are consistent with this research, as
it was rare for a contract worker to acquire a job
without a referral, a relationship with the person
hiring, or a relationship with a broker working on
the contract worker’s behalf. However, as Zuck-
erman and colleagues (2003: 1024) pointed out,
studies of how people use social networks to
acquire a job have not usually addressed the de-
gree to which a candidate’s qualifications overlap
with job requirements. Our data suggested that
for a referral to help a contractor acquire stretch-
work, the referral had to confirm the individual’s
abilities or offer assurance that those abilities
were easily transferable to new areas. We thus
inferred that for a referral to help a contract
worker obtain stretchwork, it had to provide a
source of continuity to bridge from a contractor’s
demonstrated skills to a new assignment.
Framing and bluffing. Framing one’s capabili-
ties, if not outright bluffing, was also used to ac-

framed their prior experience in terms that allowed
for easy translation to the desired jobs. Contract
workers crafted descriptions of their experience
carefully to lead potential employers to link areas
in which the workers had already demonstrated
competence to areas in which they were less profi-
cient—often using analogies or comparisons that
stressed similarities to proven skills.

In the film industry, crew members also worked
at a discount to acquire jobs for which they cur-

shared the expectation that employers would recip-

Contract workers used the four tactics described
above—differentiating competence, acquiring refer-

stretchwork often used these tactics in concert. For
instance, film crew described situations in which
they worked at a discount in order to demonstrate
their commitment to someone with the potential to
offer future stretchwork opportunities. One gaffer
explained why she was working below rate on a
project: “The only reason I took this job is out of

loyalty to Frank [the cinematographer]. He is really
fantastic to work with.” Because she wanted Frank
to continue to refer her for work on future projects
that could take her skills in new directions, she was
willing to work at a lower rate on this project.
Similarly, once Aidan proved his graphics compe-
tence to his friends, they were willing to refer him
for paid work, illustrating the relationship between
differentiating competence and obtaining referrals.

Industry Conditions

Nature of the broker relationship. Even though
both types of contract workers depended on bro-
kers for assignments, brokering relationships dif-
fered in the two industries, and this difference af-
fected a worker’s ability to be progressively mobile.
Referrals from brokers helped both types of con-
tract workers acquire stretchwork, but in the case of
film crew, referrals could also constrain their abil-
ity to pursue skill-extending work, because refer-
rals came with obligations.
The relationship between brokers and high-tech-
nology
contractors
was
contractual.
Although
many contractors maintained cordial relations with
their brokers, they fully understood that the brokers
were making money from their assignments and
did not feel obligated to them. Brokers worked for a
profit: clients paid the broker who made a match,
and he or she earned a proportion of the contrac-
tor’s wages for the assignment’s duration. Thus,
brokers had an incentive to facilitate stretchwork
assignments if they would command a higher rate.
On the other hand, extending contracts increased
profit margins, because agencies could spread
search costs over longer periods. Brokers therefore
had little financial incentive to help contractors
abandon current assignments for new ones. When

By all means sign up with several agencies. Maybe

Explicit recognition of a broker’s financial inter-
ests gave a contractor license to act opportunisti-

placement, the highest wage rate, and a job that
promised the opportunity to stretch skills. Al-
though brokers and contractors depended upon
each other for revenue, they lacked a strong sense
of mutual obligation.
The situation was precisely the reverse on the
film sets. Here, brokering was informal rather than
contractual. But because brokering was embedded
in the work itself, it resulted in a strong web of
mutual obligation. As Laura’s production manager
demonstrated by whispering in the ear of the per-
son who referred the errant casting coordinator,
when a broker worked on the same set as the re-
ferred and the referee, relationships could be easily
activated. Film crew relied on these relationships
and felt that refusing jobs, even those that did not
meet their requirements for skill progression, was
particularly damaging. To maintain relationships
with important members of the referral network,
crew members frequently accepted undesirable
jobs. A sound mixer explained why he took a job at
below market rate despite the fact that it lacked a
stretchwork opportunity:

I really felt like I had to take it because the director
used to mostly use this guy Dan and another guy
John as his sound guys, but Dan pissed the director
off so he started using me regularly. I felt like if I
turned it down, the director might go back to using
Dan.

By accepting jobs to maintain referral relation-
ships for the long term, film crew members some-
times lost opportunities to find jobs that could
stretch their skills in the near term. The density in
the network for film production (Jones & Walsh,
1997) likely enhanced the power of brokers. In
contrast, when high-technology contractors ac-
cepted less desirable jobs, it was not because of

their relationships with their brokers, but because
they needed work.

can impose constraints. Embedded brokering rela-

strained the mobility of film crew in ways that
high-technology

High-technology contractors did not need to con-

turned down jobs offered by brokers only to find

later assignments. The contractual nature of the
brokering relationship allowed high-technology
contractors to make opportunistic transitions be-
tween brokers, but the obligations of reciprocity
precluded film crew from doing so. Given this con-
trast, we would expect that embedded brokering
relations decrease the probability of acquiring
stretchwork, primarily because the force of mutual
obligations constrains mobility.

the course of production. As an office production

I’ve gotten some calls [for other promising work]

time it takes for me to get to know a certain busi-

I want to do something else now.” Most contracts

The nature of the brokering relationship that con-

industries in which they worked. Only high-tech-
nology contractors had contractual relationships

their way onto jobs through informal relationships,
where obligations rather than cash were the coin-
age of exchange. This web of mutual obligation
could help film crew with referrals to new stretch-
work assignments, but it also limited their ability to
move from one project to another. Film industry
norms emphasized staying with a project for its
duration. Other conditions that affected a contract
worker’s odds of acquiring stretchwork were at-
tributes of his or her specific workplace. The two
most important workplace conditions that shaped a
contract worker’s ability to turn an existing assign-
ment into stretchwork were the flexibility of a cur-
rent work role and the visibility of failure.

Workplace Conditions

Flexibility of work roles. A variety of factors
shaped an assignment’s flexibility, which affected a
contract worker’s ability to transition a current as-
signment into stretchwork. For example, on non-
union film sets, where roles were less formally
prescribed, crew frequently took on new tasks to
advance their skills. As a grip explained,

It is different on big union jobs; they have more
experienced people, on a job like that you can’t get
anywhere near as close to the camera. Here [on a
low-budget movie] everyone is learning; there is a
tolerance for error.

This grip’s description of his ability to work
“close to the camera” on a low-budget film suggests
that roles that are open to interpretation foster op-
portunities for stretchwork.
Unionization was irrelevant in high-technol-
ogy contracting, but the size of a firm and the
degree of structure provided by clients affected
contractors’ ability to take their skills in new
directions once on the job. Several contractors
felt that smaller companies offered more oppor-
tunities to work on emerging technologies. As
one contractor explained,

I will go work on start-ups, new technology start-
ups, because they’re more flexible, you can create
new environments, new technology.

Flexibility on the job allowed contract workers to
take jobs for which they were qualified in new
directions. Prior research suggests that role ambi-
guity can help people develop skills in new areas
(Goodman & Goodman, 1976; Kanter, 1977/1993)
and, although the contract workers in our data set
did not prefer a lack of direction, they relished the
opportunity
that
less
structured
environments
could provide. Thus, we might expect that contract
workers would be more able to acquire stretchwork
in work environments with high role flexibility
than in work environments with highly structured
roles.
Visibility of failure. On high-profile projects—
often unionized, big-budget studio films—film
crew were less willing to pursue stretchwork than
were crew on small, low-budget projects because of
the visibility of potential failure. For example,
when the first assistant camera on one big-budget
film needed an operator to act as the first assistant
camera for the next day, he offered his crew several
opportunities to volunteer. The second author
watched three crew members turn down the oppor-
tunity, even though it could have benefited their
career progression. First camera was a specialized
job in which mistakes were immediately visible in
the “dailies” that the crew watched every night.
Not only would the crew member’s competence (or
lack thereof) be visible, but also, the project was
extremely high profile. Thus, volunteering carried
the unacceptable risk of failing in front of people
with the potential to refer one for many future
projects.
On lower-profile projects, where mistakes would
be less detrimental to their careers, film crew mem-
bers felt no such compunctions about performing
new tasks. For instance, one production assistant
described how she and other crew members took
on new roles while working on a commercial about
a water reservoir:

Achieving progression in external labor mar-
kets. As the model in Figure 1 shows, stretchwork
was the primary way in which contract workers
bridged the gap between their prior experience and
the new skills they hoped to learn on their next as-
signment. Because job assignments in external labor
markets were not sequenced in an orderly way, op-
portunities for stretchwork materialized unpredict-
ably. Consequently, a contract worker’s ability to ac-
quire stretchwork depended almost entirely on his or
her ability to make opportunistic transitions.
Differentiating one’s competence, acquiring refer-
rals, framing and bluffing, and discounting facilitated
these transitions. Although these four tactics high-
light how people actively shape their progression in
markets, attributes of the workplaces and industries
in which they work enable and constrain such activ-
ities. Reciprocal relations with brokers that were em-
bedded in the work itself and a high visibility of
failure reduced a contract worker’s willingness and
ability to use these tactics for acquiring stretchwork.
In contrast, the ability to manage the duration of a
contract and flexible work roles emboldened contract
workers to use these tactics and enabled them to use
the tactics more effectively.

DISCUSSION

We began this research by questioning what
structures careers in the absence of the organiza-
tional guidance inherent in internal labor markets.
There are few theoretical attempts to explain career
transitions in external markets, and this compara-

Our experiences in the field taught us that not all
work is equally likely to provide opportunities for
progression. The idea that every new assignment
will enrich one’s skills reflects an aspiration more
than a reality. As one contractor, a software devel-
oper, noted wistfully,

You don’t have much choice about whether you will
only work on development or not work on mainte-
nance. . . . New [software] development is pretty
rare to come by.

Contract workers soon discovered that the firms
that hired them did not have the same incentives to
develop their skills as they had for employees. Fur-
thermore, producers, suppliers, and market inter-
mediaries alike tend to use individuals’ prior expe-
riences as a proxy for capabilities, which constrains
individual options for career progression. This
helps explain why such transitions are unpredict-
able and costly.
Yet our findings show that contract workers
acted strategically to assert control over their ca-
reers and reduce these costs. By focusing on how
contract workers strove to resolve the career pro-
gression paradox, we found a common response:
acquiring stretchwork. Stretchwork allowed work-
ers to leverage continuity in their experience while
extending their skills to new areas. Contract work-
ers never knew when opportunities for stretchwork
would appear. If they “held out” for stretchwork,
they risked unemployment. If they accepted some-
thing less than stretchwork, they risked foregoing
opportunities to advance their skills should these
appear. To gain control and influence the odds of
landing prized stretchwork, contract workers dif-
ferentiated their competence, acquired referrals,
framed, bluffed, and discounted.
The little research that has been done on how
people structure their careers in external labor mar-
kets (Zuckerman et al., 2003) suggests two consid-
erations for those who might take such a step:
acquiring employability by seeking diverse em-
ployment experiences and projecting coherence by
building skills that are consistent with well-recog-
nized categories. But how people might pursue
these strategies has not been fully explored. Our
contribution to this emerging literature has been to
specify more precisely how opportunity structures,
“sensemaking,” and action interconnect in the
search for career progression.
Our empirical findings show that contract work-
ers achieved employability by differentiating their

competence, acquiring referrals, and discounting.
For example, the camera operator quoted above on
the importance of “hitting his mark” not only dif-
ferentiated his competence on a particular project,
but also reassured potential future employers on
the set that he could be competent on future
projects that might require new skills. Our infor-
mants increased their referral networks to broaden
their options by differentiating their competence to
potential brokers. In some cases, they even dis-
counted the pay for their work to obtain jobs that
would enhance their skills.
But contract workers also felt that coherence mat-
tered to their job prospects. Framing, bluffing, and
referrals could substitute for experience, providing
an appearance of continuity that helped contract
workers acquire stretchwork. For instance, Adam
bluffed his way on to a job using a quality assur-
ance tool he had not used before by framing his
experience in light of the new project. Framing and
bluffing directly enhanced the appearance of coher-
ence. Our empirical data on the tactics that contract
workers used to acquire stretchwork makes three
theoretical contributions that further a theoretical
understanding of how people progress in their ca-
reers in external labor markets.

Contributions

Employability and mobility in external labor
markets. Understanding why and how contract
workers obtain stretchwork refines Baker and Al-
drich’s (1996) proposition that experience with
multiple employers facilitates the accumulation of
portable skills and enhances an individual’s mar-
ketability and thus, mobility. Our informants’ ex-
periences show that mobility did not necessarily
entail progression. Many contract workers worked
for multiple employers but repeatedly performed
the same function. By examining how people try to
resolve the career progression paradox in external
labor markets, we offer a more nuanced under-
standing of the relationship between mobility and
progression. More specifically, we found that con-
tract workers had to act strategically to be mobile in
a way that was progressive.
The notion of portable skills reflects assumptions
about employability: in particular, that skills are
uncontested attributes that port easily from one
context to another. Our data show that porting
skills to new assignments is rarely automatic and
often involves hidden work of a social nature.
Workers make an effort to demonstrate new com-
petencies in the marketplace. Learning a skill is
insufficient to make it portable; in addition, porta-
bility often rests on prior differentiation in the mar-

Coherence and control in external labor mar-
kets. Our empirical evidence helps clarify how
people who work in external labor markets respond
to concerns about coherence. Although other stud-

progression

To assert control over careers in which mobility
and progression do not go hand in hand, contract
workers take action to suggest coherence to poten-
tial employers. Because job assignments that offer
progression are unpredictably sequenced, uncer-
tainty about what job will come next heightens the
importance of opportunistic transitions. Moreover,
employers do not usually have enough experience
with a specific contract worker to use more than
surface evidence of skill to determine whether the
person can handle the requirements of a job. Our
informants
knew
how

screened

To make transitions that led to career progress
under these conditions, contract workers had little
choice but to frame their prior experience and use
referrals to project coherence. Projecting coherence
might require contract workers to address various
discontinuities in the eyes of employers. For in-
stance, new skills may encompass greater depth in
a similar content area or require additional breadth
across new content areas. Although we showed
how contractors tried to construct coherence to ac-
quire new opportunities, future research should
examine how strategies might differ depending on
the types of skills individuals are looking to
acquire.
Refining approaches to career progression. Fi-
nally, our findings suggest that if scholars are to
develop a richer understanding of how careers are
structured in external labor markets, greater atten-
tion needs to be paid to the relationship between
action and structure. Prior research on career de-

velopment has shown that individual initiative and
responsibility are important for career progression
(Baldwin & Padgett, 1993; Dechant, 1990; McCall,
1998). Our findings suggest that individual charac-
teristics are just one of several factors influencing
progressive assignments in external labor markets
and that they may be necessary but insufficient to
create progression in the presence of market or
workplace constraints. Further investigation of
how workplace and market structures interact with
individual agency would enhance theories of pro-
gression. Future exploration of such questions as,
How do market structures relate to the formation of
individual
preferences
for
skill
development?,
would offer an opportunity for micro and macro
organizational scholars to collaborate to better un-
derstand the links between market structure and
individual action.
Our comparative ethnographic approach also of-
fers useful new constructs and new contextual vari-
ables for inclusion in future research on progres-
sion in external labor markets. For example, we
showed that the temporal and contractual structure
of an industry and differences in workplace set-
tings can constrain or enable the ability of contract
workers to find skill-extending work. Prior work
within the film industry found few differences be-
tween the career outcomes of individuals in inter-
nal and external labor markets over time (Zucker-
man, 2005). Our comparison of two industries
showed that although the job ladders that link op-
portunities for progression were missing in exter-
nal labor markets, other structures were present
that could explain Zuckerman’s findings. In an in-
dustry such as film, brokers are deeply embedded
in the work, and workers have little discretion over
contract duration, which can constrain the likeli-
hood of acquiring stretchwork. One implication is
that the structure of an industry and an employ-
ment setting may matter as much as the presence or
absence of an internal labor market. In future stud-
ies of both internal and external labor markets,
researchers would be remiss if they did not con-
sider workplace and industry structures as poten-
tial predictors of career progression.

Limitations and Future Research Directions

The common tactics used to acquire stretchwork
across our two settings give us confidence that our
observations may be generalizable. However, we
focused on highly skilled, specialized occupations.
Future research should investigate how stretch-
work is obtained in other industries to determine
what tactics are reproduced in these industries.
Moreover, we have argued that the career progres-

sion paradox is particularly relevant for contract
workers, as they continuously move from project to
project. Although our investigation of how contract
workers confront the career progression paradox
has implications for theorists, practitioners, and
policymakers interested in project or contract
work, our findings may also apply to traditional
careers that are likely to move through several
organizations.
This study only analyzed how contract workers
tried to make one type of transition—to a stretch-
work assignment—and does not offer insight as to
how such transitions are sustained over time.
Hence, we cannot draw firm conclusions about
what may constitute a successful career in the long
term. Longitudinal research on the types of conti-
nuity that facilitate transitions and career develop-
ment could extend these findings. Ultimately, to
unpack
more

patterns,
scholars
should try to ascertain whether stretchwork can
enable steady progress without regression or pla-
teauing (Allen, Russell, Poteet, & Dobbins, 1999;
Ference, Stoner, & Warren, 1977) or whether regres-
sion is inevitable.
Finally, we encourage future students of project
and contract-based careers to look for patterns of
interaction between individuals’ conceptions of
skill development and their opportunity structures.
The boundaryless career construct emphasizes the
importance of fostering individually based defini-
tions of success and developing competence (De-
Fillippi & Arthur, 1994; Eby, Butts, & Lockwood,
2003). What has been less appreciated is that fur-
thering competence in a market may require new
strategies to respond not only to the lack of organ-
izational guidance in the structuring of one’s career
but also to the lack of incentives that employers
have to further it. We agree with the boundaryless
career theorists that, in external labor markets, ca-
reer progression will depend upon skill and knowl-
edge improvements. However, our study suggests
that such competencies do not stand on their own
merits and are difficult to obtain and to leverage.
Future investigations should critically examine
how these competencies interact with the extant
market structures that affect progression to under-
stand how all workers—both contract and perma-
nent—manage the career progression paradox.

APPENDIX B

Two-Stage Comparative Ethnographic Analysis

Stage 1: Emic Analysis

First, we independently performed an emic analysis, de-
veloping an insider’s perspective on the work and careers
in our contract settings. In the process of data gathering and
analysis, the first author had developed an interest in the
career strategies of high-technology contractors, and she
focused her initial coding of the data on this theme. Simi-
larly, the second author had realized, through her data
collection and analysis, that career structures and choices
played a significant role in the coordination of film work.
This parallel finding propelled the authors to collaborate
and develop a comparative theory of tactics that contract
workers use to progress in their careers.

Stage 2: Etic Analysis

In the second stage of the analysis, we reexamined data
categories that were important to both settings. Because
each author examined a different set of data, this analysis
was sustained through weekly meetings (and inter-
spersed e-mail discussions) in which we modified our
categories as we exchanged memos and commentaries.
We cycled our analyses across the data, our emerging
theoretical categories, and the relevant literature to refine
our conclusions (Miles & Huberman, 1994). We followed
this approach throughout this stage of data analysis,
which entailed developing our emic understandings into
an etic, more generalizable understanding of how con-
tract workers achieve progression.
The etic stage of analysis comprised four phases. In the
first phase, we independently built categories focused on
acquiring work that contract workers had not performed
before (as an indicator of career progression). With com-
parison of our data, it became clear that our informants
shared a desire for work that enhanced their skills. Some
of our informants referred to this as work that “stretched
them,” “took them to a new place,” or helped them
develop new competencies that would enhance their
prospects on the open market. From these data, we de-
veloped the construct of “stretchwork,” discussed in the
results section, to distinguish this type of work from “just
another assignment.” Armed with our rich contextual
understanding of the challenges of managing a contract
work career, we then realized why stretchwork was im-
portant to contract workers: it helped them to manage
what we termed the “career progression paradox.”
After specifying the meaning of this construct and
distinguishing it from simple job acquisition, in the sec-
ond phase of analysis we identified how our informants
acquired stretchwork and how these tactics differed in
our two settings. For instance, we had separately devel-
oped categories related to skill or competence on the job.
In high-technology contracting, this category contained
examples of the importance of differentiating technical
expertise. In film projects, although skill was considered
an element of competence, many examples focused on
demonstrating reliability and exceptional “go-getter”
